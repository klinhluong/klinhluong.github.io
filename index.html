TRANSMISSION 00 - THE GOLDEN RECORD, REISSUED

FROM OUTWARD TO INWARD

In 1977, NASA launched Voyager 1 and 2, each carrying a golden phonograph record. Curated by Carl Sagan and Ann Druyan, the record contained 55 greetings in 55 languages , sounds of Earth, and 116 images. It was a transmission sent outward - a message to whatever intelligence might find it.

Forty-nine years later, the question has reversed. We are no longer asking what we should send to the stars. We are asking what is vanishing here, on Earth, that we should record before it disappears.

A language dies every two weeks. Of the roughly 7,000 languages spoken today, nearly half may be gone by the end of this century. With each one, an entire way of seeing the world goes silent.

This is the Earth Signal Archive. A record of five disappearing voices. Transmitted from Earth, May 2026.

♦001 Ainu

ARCHIVE ENTRY 001 — AINU

アイヌ
AYNU ITAK

Spoken on the island of Hokkaido in northern Japan, Ainu is a language isolate — meaning it has no known relatives in any language family on Earth. When it goes silent, an entire branch of human thought goes with it.

In 1899, the Japanese government passed the Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act, banning the Ainu language and forcing children into Japanese-language schools. By 2008, only two native speakers remained. By 2021, linguists declared the language "dormant."

"Ainu" comes from the word aynu, meaning "human."

LANGUAGE FAMILY: Isolate (no known relatives)

REGION: Hokkaido, Japan

NATIVE SPEAKERS: ~2 (2025)

STATUS: Critically endangered / Dormant

BANNED: 1899 — Hokkaido Former Aborigines Protection Act

REVITALIZATION: Upopoy National Museum, opened 2020

◐002 Arem

ARCHIVE ENTRY 002 — AREM

AREM
CMBRAU

Spoken in a small area on both sides of the Laos-Vietnam border, in Quảng Bình Province. The Arem people call themselves Cmbrau. They were unknown to the outside world until 1959, when the Vietnamese military first encountered them in the forest.

In 1960, the entire population was 53 people: 30 men, 23 women. By 1999, the population had grown to 102 — but only about a quarter still used the Arem language regularly. The community was relocated from their forest homes to government resettlement centers fifty years ago, accelerating the language's decline.

Arem has a feature linguists call the sesquisyllable — "one-and-a-half syllables" — a rare phonetic structure preserved in this small community.

LANGUAGE FAMILY: Austroasiatic / Vietic / Chut

REGION: Quảng Bình, Vietnam / Khammouan, Laos

NATIVE SPEAKERS: ~40 (used regularly)

STATUS: Critically endangered

FIRST DOCUMENTED: 1959

WRITING SYSTEM: None

●003 Taushiro

ARCHIVE ENTRY 003 — TAUSHIRO

TAUSHIRO
PINCHI

Spoken in the Peruvian Amazon, in the village of Intuto on the banks of the Tigre River, Loreto. Once thousands of people spoke Taushiro. By the late 1990s, only a small handful of tribe members remained, hiding in a booby-trapped camp on a bend of the "wild river." Today, one person speaks the language: Amadeo García García.

In 1999, Amadeo's brother Juan lay dying of malaria in the jungle. Amadeo hauled him into a canoe and rushed to reach the clinic in Intuto. At the dock, Juan drifted in and out of consciousness. He looked up at Amadeo one last time and spoke.

Ta va'a ui. — I am dying.

These were the last words ever spoken in Taushiro between two living speakers. The church bell rang that afternoon. From that moment, Amadeo became the last.

Now Amadeo lives alone in a clapboard house behind the town's water tower. Each morning, he sits on his porch and reads aloud the only literature ever written in Taushiro: verses of the Bible, translated by missionaries who came to convert his tribe years ago.

LANGUAGE FAMILY: Isolate (no known relatives)

REGION: Intuto, Loreto, Peru

NATIVE SPEAKERS: 1

STATUS: Moribund

LAST SPEAKER: Amadeo García García

LAST EXCHANGE: 1999, between brothers

DOCUMENTATION: 1,500 words, 27 stories, 3 songs
(Peru Ministry of Culture)

▶004 Tanema

ARCHIVE ENTRY 004 — TANEMA

TANEMA
TETAWO

Spoken on the small island of Vanikoro in the Solomon Islands, closer to Vanuatu than to the rest of the country. Once, three languages divided the island: Teanu in the northeast, Lovono in the west, Tanema in the south. By 2021, Lovono was extinct. Tanema survives in the memory of one man: Lainol Nalo.

In 1788, two French ships under the navigator Lapérouse wrecked on Vanikoro's reef. The story of those ships entered Vanikoro oral tradition, told in all three languages of the island. In 2005, linguist Alexandre François recorded elderly storyteller Emele Mamuli telling the legend in Tanema. She said the ships were destroyed by the Gods of the island.

Toñaki ake Laperus. — The ship of Lapérouse.

By 2012, all the older Tanema speakers had passed. Only Lainol Nalo remained.

LANGUAGE FAMILY: Austronesian / Oceanic / Temotu

REGION: Vanikoro, Solomon Islands

NATIVE SPEAKERS: 1

STATUS: Moribund

LAST SPEAKER: Lainol Nalo

DOCUMENTATION: Pangloss Collection, CNRS (2021)

NEIGHBORING LANGUAGES: Teanu (active), Lovono (extinct 2021)

▪005 Yagan

ARCHIVE ENTRY 005 — YAGAN

YAGAN
YÁMANA

Spoken at the southernmost tip of South America, in Tierra del Fuego — closer to Antarctica than to most of Chile. The Yagan people were maritime nomads, traveling the freezing channels in bark canoes. They had inhabited this region for over 10,000 years.

On February 16, 2022, Cristina Calderón — known as Abuela Cristina, the grandmother — died in a hospital in Punta Arenas of COVID-19 complications. She was 93. With her, the last fluent voice of Yagan went silent.

The Yagan language is famous on the internet for one word: mamihlapinatapai — listed in the 1994 Guinness Book of Records as the world's "most succinct word," romantically translated as "a look shared between two people, each wishing the other would do something both desire but neither will begin."

Yet the word's romantic meaning sits uneasily with Cristina's own knowledge of her language. The internet had carried mamihlapinatapai further than the language itself.

"I'm the last speaker of Yagan. Others can understand it, but don't speak it or know it like I do." — Cristina Calderón, 2017

LANGUAGE FAMILY: Isolate (no known relatives)

REGION: Tierra del Fuego, Chile

NATIVE SPEAKERS: 0 fluent

STATUS: Extinct (native fluency)

LAST FLUENT SPEAKER: Cristina Calderón (1928-2022)

RECOGNITION: Chilean "Living Human Treasure" (2009)

DOCUMENTATION: Bridges' Yahgan-English dictionary, ~30,000 words (1860s-1880s)
